Beyond GPS: Understanding Your Phone's Satellite Connection

We typically say "I'm using GPS," but this is technically incorrect. GPS is just one brand of satellite network owned by the United States. Modern smartphones are actually Multi-GNSS devices (Global Navigation Satellite Systems), meaning they connect to up to four different space networks simultaneously to pinpoint your location.

The Big Four Constellations

Your phone constantly scans the sky for signals from these networks:

1. GPS (Global Positioning System)

  • Owner: USA (Space Force)
  • Satellites: ~31 operational
  • Strengths: The original global standard. Highly reliable and integrated into every chipset.

2. GLONASS

  • Owner: Russia (Roscosmos)
  • Satellites: ~24 operational
  • Strengths: High-latitude performance. Because of its orbital inclination, GLONASS is often more accurate than GPS if you are in Canada, Scandinavia, or Northern Europe.

3. Galileo

  • Owner: European Union (ESA)
  • Satellites: ~26 operational
  • Strengths: Civilian precision. Unlike GPS and GLONASS which were built for military use first, Galileo is civilian-controlled. It offers better accuracy (down to 1 meter) for commercial devices.

4. BeiDou

  • Owner: China
  • Satellites: ~35 operational
  • Strengths: Extremely dense coverage in Asia and the Pacific, though now globally available.

Why "Multi-Band" Matters (L1 vs. L5)

Newer phones (like the iPhone 14/15 Pro and Samsung S24) utilize Dual-Frequency GPS.

Older phones only listened to the L1 frequency. This signal bounces off skyscrapers easily, causing "Multipath Errors" where your blue dot jumps across the street in a city.

New phones listen to L5 (a newer, stronger frequency) in addition to L1. The L5 signal passes through clouds and trees better and is easier for the phone to distinguish from a reflection. This allows TellMyLocation.com to achieve 3-meter accuracy even in "Urban Canyons" like New York City or Tokyo.
