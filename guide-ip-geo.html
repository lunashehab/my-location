The Science of IP Geolocation: How the Internet Finds You

Every device connected to the internet is assigned a unique identifier known as an Internet Protocol (IP) Address (e.g., 192.168.1.1). But unlike a GPS chip, an IP address does not inherently know where it is physically located.

So, how do websites know which city you are in? They rely on massive databases maintained by Regional Internet Registries (RIRs).

1. The Registry Hierarchy (WHOIS)

The world is divided into five zones managed by specific organizations. When an Internet Service Provider (ISP) like Comcast or AT&T buys a "block" of 100,000 IP addresses, they must register that block with the local authority:

  • ARIN: North America
  • RIPE NCC: Europe, Middle East, Central Asia
  • APNIC: Asia Pacific
  • LACNIC: Latin America
  • AFRINIC: Africa

The ISP fills out a "WHOIS" record, declaring: "This block of IPs will be used in Atlanta, Georgia." That declaration creates the initial map data.

2. The "Last Mile" Problem

This system is imperfect. ISPs frequently move blocks of IP addresses to different cities to balance network load. If a block registered in Atlanta is suddenly re-routed to serve customers in Savannah, the database might take weeks to update.

This is why you might visit a weather site on your desktop computer (which has no GPS) and see the weather for a city 50 miles away. The site isn't broken; the ISP's record is just stale.

3. VPNs and Proxies: Intentional Obfuscation

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) works by tunneling your traffic through a server in a different location. If you are in London but connect to a VPN server in New York, websites see the IP address of the New York server, not your home router.

This effectively "teleports" your digital identity. TellMyLocation.com can detect the location of that exit node, but we cannot see through the encryption tunnel to your true physical location without hardware GPS permission.

4. The Solution: Hardware Verification

Because IP Geolocation is an estimation (Accuracy: ~80%), mission-critical applications rely on Hardware GPS. By asking for browser permission (`navigator.geolocation`), we bypass the IP database entirely and ask your device's actual silicon chip where it is located relative to satellites.
