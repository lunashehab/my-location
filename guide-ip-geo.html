The Science of IP Geolocation

Every device connected to the internet is assigned a unique number known as an IP Address (e.g., 192.168.1.1). But how does a website turn that number into a physical location on a map?

The WHOIS Database

When an Internet Service Provider (ISP) like Comcast or AT&T buys a block of IP addresses, they register them with a regional authority (like ARIN in North America). This registration often includes the city where that block of IPs is intended to be used.

Why It is Often Inaccurate

IP Geolocation is a "best guess." Here is why it might show you in the wrong city:

  • The "Headquarters" Problem: An ISP might register all their IPs to their corporate HQ in a major city, even if the user is 500 miles away in a rural town.
  • Dynamic IPs: ISPs frequently shuffle IP addresses between neighborhoods. A database might think an IP is in New York, but it was re-assigned to New Jersey yesterday.
  • VPNs and Proxies: These tools intentionally route your traffic through a server in a different country to mask your true location.

The Solution: Hardware GPS

This is why TellMyLocation.com asks for "Location Permission." We bypass the inaccurate IP method and ask your device's actual GPS chip for the truth. This improves accuracy from "City Level" (IP) to "Street Level" (GPS).
