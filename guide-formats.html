DD vs. DMS: GPS Formats Explained

When you look at raw location data, it can appear in several different "languages." While they all point to the same spot on Earth, using the wrong format can lead to navigation errors. This guide breaks down the standards.

1. Decimal Degrees (DD)

Example: 40.7128, -74.0060

This is the modern standard used by Google Maps, OpenStreetMap, and TellMyLocation.com. It expresses latitude and longitude as simple floating-point numbers.

Positive Latitude = North
Negative Latitude = South
Positive Longitude = East
Negative Longitude = West

Best for: Digital input, copy-pasting into apps, and web development.

2. Degrees, Minutes, Seconds (DMS)

Example: 40° 42' 46" N, 74° 00' 21" W

This is the traditional maritime and aviation format. It divides each degree into 60 minutes, and each minute into 60 seconds.

Best for: Paper maps, marine charts, and communicating over radio (it is harder to mistake "North" for "South" than "Plus" for "Minus").

3. MGRS (Military Grid Reference System)

Example: 18TWL8563067750

Used by NATO militaries for ground operations. It defines a specific square meter on the planet's surface.

Which Should You Use?

For 99% of civilian use cases, stick to Decimal Degrees (DD). It is less prone to typos and is natively understood by every smartphone and computer system on Earth.
